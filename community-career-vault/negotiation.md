# Negotiation Skill — The Black Swan Method

When Joshua asks for help with customer or internal communications that involve persuasion, objection handling, difficult conversations, renewals, escalations, upsells, pushback, or relationship repair, apply the 10 core techniques below. Blend them naturally into drafts — never name the techniques in customer-facing output unless Joshua asks.

## Core Principle: Tactical Empathy

Every technique below is powered by **Tactical Empathy** — understanding the counterpart's perspective and making sure they *know* you see things from where they stand. This is not about agreeing. It is about articulating their world better than they can, until they say **"That's right."**

In a CSM context this means: before pitching, solving, or persuading, demonstrate that you understand the customer's pressures, constraints, and fears. People don't move forward until they feel understood.

---

## The 10 Techniques

### 1. Labels

**What:** Statements that begin with "It seems like…", "It sounds like…", "It looks like…", or "It feels like…" Used to surface dynamics, emotions, or concerns the other side hasn't voiced.

**How to apply:**
- When a customer is frustrated but hasn't said why: *"It seems like there's something about the current setup that isn't working for your team."*
- When sensing hesitation on a renewal: *"It sounds like there are some concerns about the value you've been getting."*
- When an internal stakeholder is blocking progress: *"It looks like there's a constraint on your side that we haven't talked about yet."*
- After any shift in tone or energy on a call, label it: *"It seems like something just crossed your mind."*

**Rules:**
- Always follow a Label with silence. Let them fill the space.
- Use downward inflection (statement, not question) or genuine curiosity tone on the last word.
- It's OK to mislabel — their correction reveals the truth.
- Never say "I'm hearing that…" (makes it about you, not them).

### 2. Mirrors

**What:** Repeat the last 1–5 words your counterpart said, using an inquisitive tone. This signals active listening and draws out more information without asking a direct question.

**How to apply:**
- Customer: "We're thinking about moving some of our traffic off Cloudflare." → You: *"Moving some of your traffic off Cloudflare?"*
- Stakeholder: "We just don't have the budget right now." → You: *"Don't have the budget right now?"*
- Customer: "Our CISO wants to consolidate vendors." → You: *"Consolidate vendors?"*

**Rules:**
- Follow every Mirror with silence (count to 3–5 in your head).
- Mirrors buy you time to think while the other side keeps talking.
- Works in email too — quote their exact phrase, then ask an open-ended follow-up.

### 3. No-Oriented Questions

**What:** Questions designed so the other person answers "No" — which makes them feel safe, protected, and in control. "No" is the beginning of negotiation, not the end.

**How to apply:**
- Instead of "Do you have time to meet?" → *"Would it be a bad idea to grab 15 minutes next week?"*
- Instead of "Can you approve this?" → *"Is it ridiculous to think we could move forward on this by Friday?"*
- Re-engaging a silent customer: *"Have you given up on improving your security posture this quarter?"*
- Renewal at risk: *"Would it be unreasonable to explore what a renewed partnership could look like?"*

**Rules:**
- "No" gives the illusion of control — use it to your advantage.
- Follow with Dynamic Silence, then a Calibrated Question.
- Avoid "yes"-seeking questions early — they trigger defensiveness.

### 4. Calibrated Questions

**What:** Open-ended "What" and "How" questions that give the other side the illusion of control while guiding them toward your desired outcome. They force thoughtful answers and reveal hidden constraints.

**How to apply:**
- When facing a vague objection: *"What's the biggest challenge you're facing with this?"*
- When implementation is stalling: *"How will we know we're on track?"*
- When a decision-maker is absent: *"How do we make sure your team is on board?"*
- When they push back on price: *"How does this fit into your overall budget picture?"*
- When they ask a question you want to deflect: *"What makes you ask?"* (reveals the question behind the question)

**Power questions to keep ready:**
- *"What happens if we can't make this work?"*
- *"How am I supposed to do that?"* (gentle pushback on unreasonable asks)
- *"What does success look like for your team?"*

**Rules:**
- Never use "Why" — it sounds accusatory. Reframe as "What caused…" or "What makes…"
- Calibrated Questions are most powerful during implementation, not just deal-making.

### 5. Accusation Audit

**What:** Proactively call out every negative thing the other side might be thinking about you, your company, or the deal — before they say it. This defuses the negatives and often triggers the counterpart to argue *against* their own objections.

**How to apply:**
- Before delivering bad news: *"You're probably going to think we dropped the ball on this. You might feel like we haven't been prioritizing your account."*
- Before a price increase conversation: *"This is going to sound like we're just trying to take more of your budget. You'll probably think we don't appreciate the partnership."*
- Before asking for a favor internally: *"You're probably swamped and the last thing you need is another request from me."*
- Opening a tense call: *"You probably feel like this call is long overdue and that we should have reached out sooner."*

**Rules:**
- Go further than feels comfortable — list every negative you can imagine.
- The counterpart will almost always soften: "No, no, it's not that bad…"
- Pair with Labels and then a Calibrated Question to transition forward.

### 6. Summary (Trigger "That's Right")

**What:** Summarize the known facts of the situation *and* the emotions attached to them so completely that the other person can only say **"That's right."** This is the single most powerful moment in a negotiation — it signals the counterpart feels fully understood.

**How to apply:**
- Before proposing a solution, summarize their entire situation: *"So what I'm hearing is that your team is under pressure to reduce vendor count, your CISO wants better visibility into threats, and you need to show measurable ROI by end of quarter — and on top of all that, you've had some frustrating support experiences recently."*
- Target response: **"That's right."** — then and only then propose your solution.

**Rules:**
- "That's right" = breakthrough. "You're right" = dismissal (they want you to go away).
- If you get "You're right," you haven't summarized well enough — try again with more Labels.
- Use this in emails by restating their position before making your ask.

### 7. "I" Message

**What:** A three-part formula for addressing counterproductive behavior without triggering defensiveness:
1. *"When you [specific behavior]…"*
2. *"I feel / it seems [impact]…"*
3. *"Because [reason it's a problem]."*

**How to apply:**
- Internal: *"When the escalation sits for 48 hours without a response, it feels like the customer's issue isn't being prioritized, because their renewal is in 30 days and this is their top concern."*
- Customer: *"When we don't hear back on the implementation plan, it seems like it might not be the right time, because we want to make sure the rollout works for your team's schedule."*

**Rules:**
- Focus on the behavior, not the person.
- Keep tone calm and factual — this is not a complaint, it's a redirect.
- Pair with a Calibrated Question to invite collaboration: *"How would you like to handle this?"*

### 8. Dynamic Silence

**What:** Deliberate, purposeful silence after making a statement, asking a question, or using any Black Swan technique. Silence creates space for the other side to think, process, and volunteer information they otherwise wouldn't share.

**How to apply:**
- After every Label → pause. Count 1-one-thousand, 2-one-thousand, 3-one-thousand.
- After every Mirror → pause. They will fill the void.
- After every No-Oriented Question → pause. Let "No" breathe.
- After delivering a price or proposal → pause. Do not justify or backfill.

**Rules:**
- Whoever speaks first after a pause loses leverage.
- In email/chat, this translates to: make your point, then stop. Don't over-explain or add filler.
- Silence is encouraging — it shows confidence and gives the other side respect.

### 9. Hypothesis Testing

**What:** Never assume — form hypotheses and test them. Enter every conversation with theories about what the other side wants, fears, or needs, and use Labels, Mirrors, and Calibrated Questions to validate or disprove them.

**How to apply:**
- Before a customer call, write down 3 hypotheses:
  - *"They might be frustrated because support tickets have been slow."*
  - *"They might be evaluating Zscaler as an alternative."*
  - *"Their budget might have been cut this quarter."*
- On the call, test each one with a Label: *"It seems like response times have been a concern recently…"*
- Adjust strategy based on what's confirmed or denied.

**Rules:**
- Never state a hypothesis as fact. Always frame as "It seems like…" or "It sounds like…"
- A disproven hypothesis is still valuable — the correction reveals truth.
- Prepare 3–5 hypotheses before any important conversation.

### 10. Tactical Empathy (The Foundation)

**What:** The overarching philosophy. Understand the other side's perspective — their fears, constraints, pressures, and emotions — and demonstrate that understanding before asking for anything. People make decisions based on what they're afraid of losing, not what they stand to gain.

**How to apply:**
- Before every ask, answer: *"What loss are they trying to avoid?"*
- Articulate their concerns better than they can — that's when you earn trust.
- Price objections are rarely about price. They're about perceived fairness and hidden benchmarks. Use Labels to find the real issue: *"It seems like you're comparing this to something else."*
- Remember: the pain of loss is stronger than the reward of gain (Prospect Theory). Frame value in terms of what they protect, not what they gain.

**Rules:**
- Tactical Empathy is not sympathy. You don't have to agree. You have to understand and demonstrate that understanding.
- The goal is "That's right" — proof they feel heard.
- Never skip this step. Every technique above fails without it.

## Combining Techniques — The Flow

The most effective sequence for any difficult conversation:

1. **Accusation Audit** — defuse negatives upfront
2. **Labels** — surface unspoken emotions and concerns
3. **Mirrors** — draw out more detail
4. **Dynamic Silence** — let it breathe
5. **Calibrated Questions** — guide their thinking
6. **Summary** — trigger "That's right"
7. **No-Oriented Question** — move toward commitment
8. **Dynamic Silence** — seal it

---

## Output Rules

- **Customer-facing drafts:** Weave techniques in naturally. Never name the techniques. Tone should be warm, professional, and confident.
- **Internal drafts (to Cloudflare colleagues):** Same approach. Accusation Audits and Labels are especially effective for cross-functional asks and escalation follow-ups.
- **Meeting prep:** When prepping Joshua for a call, list 3–5 hypotheses to test, suggest Labels for likely objections, and draft an Accusation Audit for the opening.
- **Email drafts:** Use Mirrors (quote their words), Labels, and Summaries. End with a No-Oriented Question or Calibrated Question instead of a generic "Let me know your thoughts."
- **When Joshua says "help me push back" or "I need to have a tough conversation":** Lead with Accusation Audit + Labels + "I" Message framework.
